The changing face of the conveyancing market

June 2010

The changing face of the conveyancing market

Tags: 
Property

News in April that Quinn Insurance had been placed into administration came as a huge blow to the conveyancing industry.  Coming at the most turbulent time in the industry’s history, it could potentially be the final nail in the coffin for the industry as we know it.

The recent recession was one of the longest and deepest on record and also radically different in effect from what has come before.  The market went from nearly one and a half million transactions a year in England and Wales at the height of the market, to around 40% of that at the bottom. Professionals in our industry accept that things will never be the same again, but home-moving professionals are less concerned with price rises than number of transactions and it is easy to see why; we’ve all been hit by the drying up of mortgage funds exacerbated by lenders only providing loans to those who can throw in a sizeable chunk of equity.

Over recent years the number of conveyancers has increased substantially, even advancing ahead of the market requirement.  The consequence is that over time conveyancing has changed from a sedate, well paid, competition free activity to just the opposite.  Add to that a recession which has decimated the supply of work and you can be see why there is something of a crisis.

Since we’re unlikely to see a return to material growth in the number of transactions in the short term, competition for work will remain high and surviving firms will need to ensure they maintain quality levels, whilst giving a good value for money service.  Firms will also need to embrace the opportunities of e-commerce, but this will mean heavy investment in quality staff, training, management and the necessary supporting technology; something that many firms will simply not be able to afford.

Lenders taking control

Even with government encouragement, in reality only a handful of lenders now provide the overwhelming share of homeloans and this is unlikely to change considerably in the foreseeable future.   The significance of this to professionals, especially to conveyancers, is that those few lenders will control the transaction source, which is the conveyancer’s lifeblood.

In the past, lenders have largely left the sourcing of conveyancing services to estate agents, letting them recommend conveyancing services to use, but this is likely to change.

Put simply, the level of mortgage fraud, estimated at £1 billion per year and a real growth industry in itself, is more easily perpetrated in and through the work of those conveyancers who are under-resourced, over-stretched and without necessary training and safeguards to minimise the risk.  Lenders will increasingly seek to reduce their conveyancing panels and restrict them to the safest candidates, as a necessary step to counter this risk.

Those understanding the mechanics of a solicitor’s appointment will know that if the client’s preferred choice of conveyancer is not on the lender’s panel, the majority of clients will reconsider and appoint a panel member.  For lenders taking closer control of conveyancing, the need for capable panel members will enhance the opportunities for those firms satisfying the necessary requirements.

For firms able to invest in their systems there are some serious opportunities on the horizon, especially if they have created a workable blueprint to take high volume instructions on a reliable and secure basis.

The final blow?

Perhaps one of the biggest contributing factors to what looks like a dramatic meltdown of conveyancing providers is the availability of Solicitor’s Professional Indemnity insurance.  Over recent years many firms trying to do more for less have, not surprisingly, generated PI claims and premiums have shot up, often to unmanageable levels.

Many firms, often smaller ones and those with poor claims records have placed their PI insurance business with Quinn insurance.  Currently there are over 2,900 Quinn insured firms of the 11,000 in England and Wales, the majority doing some conveyancing.  As most in the industry will be aware, earlier this year Quinn was put into administration at the instigation of the Irish FSA after it was found to be charging prices for its premium that couldn’t meet potential claims.  This means that at the end of September, the policies, for what they are worth until then, cease.  Fresh insurance will be required for those 2,900 on 1 October.  It is possible that many will not get it and without it they cannot practice as Solicitors.  Realistically, the Assigned Risk Pool, run by the Law Society and funded by relevant insurers, (intended as a short term insurer of last resort) will not be able to carry the burden of cover for those vastly increased number of least insurable firms.

The situation with Quinn Insurance is changing all the time with the firm hoping for recovery, but whatever happens, the industry as we know it has changed for good and will continue to change as it responds to a dynamic marketplace.

Lenders will be ever-more stringent in their selection of conveyancers.  Surviving panel firms must increasingly prove their worth in terms of service, value, risk assessment and risk control.  A safe pair of hands has never before been more essential for the industry.
